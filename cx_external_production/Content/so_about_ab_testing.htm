About A/B Testing

Site Optimizer is used to create A/B tests on your store and is integrated with the Marketing Offer functions so that you can create and set up offers, offer related content, pricing, and product information for use in Site Optimizer Tests.

A/B testing provides a unique and effective way to optimize online store performance because it is metrics-driven, objective, and more efficient than other site optimization techniques. By creating an A/B test, you can randomly display several content, pricing, or promotional offers simultaneously on your storefront while testing the impact of each approach.

For example, if you wanted to determine the best use of a promotional banner area on your store home page, you might set up two different creative designs and show 50% of your store traffic one design, and 50% of your store traffic the other design. By running this test, you would then measure the close ratio (conversion rate) and sales revenue impact of each design. Another benefit of this approach is that you control all other factors (referring traffic source, other store promotions, etc.) because traffic is randomly assigned to each of the two test designs. This yields an objective measurement—data you can make real, quality decisions with.

What Happens During an A/B Test

When an A/B test is live on your store, the system captures key measures of performance for each level (branch) within the test and also automatically computes the statistical significance of the results. This provides the proper ability to decide on a winning test level.

Data from each A/B test level (a branch or version of the test) becomes the basis for which test level is determined the strongest or highest performing. Some common measures of test performance for an online store are: close ratio, sales, average order size, and sales per unique visitor. Close ratio and sales per unique visitor are usually the most important metrics for A/B testing because they show the actual revenue impact of your tests and allow you to compare the results of each test level even if one test level received more traffic or views than another.

Reading Test Results

When reading test results, it is critical to evaluate the statistical significance of the data in the test. The statistical significance is a measure of whether the lift or gain you are seeing in a particular test level is actually statistically valid within a certain confidence level.

Think of this as similar to election night results that are within a certain margin of error. If the results are too close to call and fall within the margin of error, you may not be able to make a determination of the winner. Statistical significance is similar to this in that if your test levels are too close across close ratio and revenue per visitor terms, you cannot make a good decision about which test level is the winner because the difference in performance of any particular are not statistically different than the other levels.
